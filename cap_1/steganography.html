Brief History Introduction

Steganography is the technique of hiding information within another message or object in a way that conceals its presence from an observer. In digital contexts, this can involve embedding a file, image, video, or message within another similar medium, making it appear unremarkable. Hidden messages often seem to be everyday items like images, articles, or shopping lists; for instance, a secret message might be written in invisible ink between the lines of a letter.

The first recorded uses of steganography can be traced back to 440 BC in Greece, when Herodotus mentions two examples in his Histories. Histiaeus sent a message to his vassal, Aristagoras, by shaving the head of his most trusted servant, "marking" the message onto his scalp, then sending him on his way once his hair had regrown, with the instruction, "When thou art come to Miletus, bid Aristagoras shave thy head, and look thereon." Additionally, Demaratus sent a warning about a forthcoming attack to Greece by writing it directly on the wooden backing of a wax tablet before applying its beeswax surface. Wax tablets were in common use then as reusable writing surfaces, sometimes used for shorthand.

Theoretical Concepts

Unlike cryptography, which only protects the content of a message, steganography conceals both the message and its existence, helping it avoid detection. Encrypted messages, while secure, can attract attention or suspicion, especially in places where encryption is restricted. Steganography has long been used to hide information in physical items, such as invisible ink on paper, Morse code on yarn, microdots, or musical ciphers that encode messages as musical notes.

In societies with censorship or cultural restrictions, people often use "cultural steganography" by embedding messages in pop culture references, idioms, or other public communications, assuming these are being monitored.

Methods for embedding messages in digital cover media have existed since the invention of computers. An unbreakable cypher, such as the one-time pad, creates ciphertexts that appear completely random without the private key. The message to be concealed is frequently encrypted and then used to overwrite a portion of a much bigger block of encrypted data or a block of random data.

Changes to pixels in image or sound files, digital text attributes like font selection and spacing, chaffing and winnowing, mimic functions, altering a sound file's echo, and adding information to parts of a file that are disregarded are a few examples.
